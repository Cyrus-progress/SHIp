Abstract

The Silica-Histidine Polyplex (SHiP) mRNA delivery platform is proposed as a structurally oxidation-resistant alternative to current ionizable lipid nanoparticles. Here we provide the first quantitative comparison of oxidation susceptibility and predicted shelf-life for SHiP versus current mRNA-LNP formulations, using published bond-dissociation energies and multi-pathway Arrhenius kinetics.

Two key findings are reported:

  1. Oxidation-initiation susceptibility of SHiP components is ~10¹³× lower than the most susceptible component of current LNP formulations. SHiP contains zero bis-allylic C–H bonds; LNP formulations contain 22 such sites per lipid set across SM-102, ALC-0315, DLin-MC3-DMA, and cholesterol.

  2. SHiP is predicted to meet a 12-month-at-25°C shelf-life target with median shelf-life 51 months (90% CI: 24–102 months) at 25°C, compared to 0.7 months (CI: 0.3–1.5) for current mRNA-LNP at the same temperature. At 40°C (tropical excursion), SHiP predicted shelf life is 9 months (CI: 4–18) versus 0.2 months for LNP.

These predictions are derived from published kinetic parameters with Monte-Carlo-propagated uncertainty (n = 10,000 samples). They support the central thesis of the SHiP platform: that removing oxidizable substrate produces a qualitative rather than incremental improvement in ambient-temperature mRNA vaccine stability.

1. Background and Motivation

Current mRNA-LNP vaccines require cold-chain storage primarily because lipid oxidation proceeds even at sub-zero temperatures through radical chain propagation at bis-allylic C–H positions (BDE ≈ 75 kcal/mol) in the unsaturated tails of ionizable lipids. This has been well- characterized mechanistically but not, to our knowledge, previously quantified as a comparative audit against proposed non-lipid alternatives.

The SHiP platform proposes replacement of the ionizable lipid with a saturated poly(histidine-co-lysine) (pHK) polymer coated by a ~5 nm biomimetic silica shell and stabilized in a trehalose glass matrix. Under the design hypothesis, no oxidizable substrate remains.

This report tests that hypothesis quantitatively and translates the result into a predicted shelf-life profile.

2. Methods

2.1 Part A — Oxidation Susceptibility Audit

For each molecular component in current mRNA-LNP formulations (SM-102, ALC-0315, DLin-MC3-DMA, cholesterol, DSPC) and in SHiP (lysine, histidine, silica shell, PEG, mannose, trehalose), we identified the weakest C–H bond dissociation energy (BDE) accessible to peroxyl-radical abstraction. BDE values were taken from Luo (2007) and Yin et al. (Chem. Rev. 2011). Site counts were derived from molecular structure.

The relative rate of oxidation initiation at 25 °C was computed by the standard Evans-Polanyi-type approximation:

k_rel = n_sites · exp(−ΔBDE / RT)

where ΔBDE is the difference between a component's weakest C–H BDE and a reference aliphatic C–H bond (98 kcal/mol, 1 site). This captures the dominant exponential sensitivity of initiation rate to bond energy at ambient temperature. Absolute rates depend on initiator concentration, oxygen partial pressure, and solvent properties; the ratio between components is the physically meaningful and stable comparison.

2.2 Part B — Multi-Pathway Shelf-Life Model

Four parallel degradation pathways were modeled as first-order decays with Arrhenius temperature dependence:

F(T, t) = Πᵢ exp[ −kᵢ(T) · t ]

where the pathways are: (i) lipid oxidation, (ii) particle aggregation, (iii) mRNA hydrolysis, and (iv) lipid hydrolysis (LNP only). For SHiP, a fifth residual-risk pathway — shell/moisture ingress — was added to honestly capture the uncertainty of the glass-matrix storage stability over multi-year timescales.

Rate constants at the reference temperature (25 °C) and activation energies were assigned from published literature:

Pathway LNP k (1/day) LNP Ea SHiP k (1/day) SHiP Ea
Lipid oxidation 3.0e-3 14 0 (eliminated) —
Particle aggregation 8.0e-4 22 1.0e-5 30
mRNA hydrolysis 1.2e-4 22 3.0e-5 22
Lipid hydrolysis 4.0e-4 18 0 (N/A) —
Shell/moisture — — 2.0e-5 12

SHiP rate constants were set conservatively:

  • mRNA hydrolysis slowdown: 4× (not 10× as sometimes claimed for optimally-dried trehalose formulations), per Ai et al. (2023) lyophilized mRNA data.
  • Particle aggregation: ~100× reduction by glass immobilization.
  • Shell/moisture ingress: included as a distinct residual-risk pathway to avoid understating degradation on multi-year timescales.

Shelf-life was defined as time to 90% potency (standard pharmaceutical spec: 10% loss). Uncertainty was propagated via Monte Carlo with 10,000 samples: rate constants drawn from log-normal distributions (±0.3 dex), activation energies from normal distributions (±2 kcal/mol).

2.3 Relationship to prior work

This framework extends the biphasic Arrhenius model developed for the author's GVRSF 2026 project, which was validated at MAE 4.52% and R² 0.848 against experimental mRNA-LNP degradation data across 36 literature data points. Extensions introduced here:

  • Multi-pathway parallelism with independent kinetic parameters per pathway.
  • SHiP-specific rate constants informed by the Part A oxidation audit.
  • Monte Carlo uncertainty propagation with reported confidence intervals.
  • Shelf-life nomogram output rather than single-temperature curves.

3. Results

3.1 Oxidation Susceptibility

Figure 1. Log-scale comparison of relative oxidation initiation rates across molecular components. Red: in current LNP formulations only. Cyan: in SHiP only. Gray: in both. Reference rate corresponds to an aliphatic C–H bond at 98 kcal/mol, one site per molecule.

Key observations:

  • Three ionizable lipids (SM-102, DLin-MC3-DMA, ALC-0315) cluster at ~10¹⁷ × the reference rate, driven by multiple bis-allylic C–H sites at ~75 kcal/mol per tail.
  • Cholesterol sits at ~10¹¹ × reference due to its allylic C–H at C7, the site of 7-ketocholesterol and 7-OH-cholesterol formation.
  • DSPC and most SHiP components (PEG, histidine, lysine, mannose, trehalose) fall between 10⁰ and 10⁴ × reference.
  • The silica shell has no C–H bonds at all. Oxidation initiation rate is effectively zero.
  • Worst-case LNP vs. worst-case SHiP component: ratio ~6.3 × 10¹³.

The count of bis-allylic C–H sites (BDE < 80 kcal/mol), which are the kinetically accessible handles for peroxyl-mediated initiation:

  • LNP formulation: 22 sites (SM-102: 8, ALC-0315: 6, DLin-MC3-DMA: 8)
  • SHiP formulation: 0 sites

This is not a matter of degree. SHiP has zero substrate for the dominant peroxidation chain mechanism.

3.2 Shelf-Life Predictions

Figure 2. Predicted shelf-life (time to 90% retained potency) as a function of storage temperature for current mRNA-LNP (red) and SHiP (cyan). Solid lines: median of 400 Monte Carlo samples. Shaded regions: 90% confidence interval. Green band indicates the SHiP design target (12 months at 25 °C).

Point predictions at canonical storage temperatures:

Storage T (°C) LNP shelf life (median) SHiP shelf life (median) Ratio
−20 72 months 160+ months 2000+×
4 5.8 months 113+ months 591×
25 0.8 months 58 months (CI: 24–102) 72×
40 0.2 months 10 months (CI: 4–18) 113×

Critical finding: The SHiP 90% confidence interval at 25 °C (24–102 months) sits entirely above the 12-month design target. Even under pessimistic assumptions, the platform is predicted to meet its stability specification.

At 40 °C, the SHiP median (10 months) is above the 6-month tropical- excursion target, but the lower bound of the CI (4 months) is below it. This is consistent with the design positioning: SHiP targets 24–25 °C pharmacy-shelf stability, not indefinite 40 °C storage.

3.3 Dominant Failure Modes

Figure 3. Fraction of total degradation rate attributable to each pathway, for LNP (left) and SHiP (right), at 25 °C.

  • Current mRNA-LNP: lipid oxidation dominates (69%), with particle aggregation (19%), lipid hydrolysis (9%), and intrinsic mRNA hydrolysis (3%) as smaller contributors.
  • SHiP: failure is distributed across residual pathways: mRNA hydrolysis (50%), shell/moisture ingress (33%), particle aggregation (17%). Total degradation rate is ~72× lower than LNP.

The shift in dominant pathway — from a chemically specific, chain- amplified mechanism (oxidation) to diffusion-limited and intrinsic processes — is the central mechanistic argument for SHiP. The remaining pathways are bounded, not autocatalytic, and are shared with every lyophilized biologic. The oxidation pathway, by contrast, has no thermodynamic floor in the current LNP chemistry.

4. Discussion

4.1 What this analysis demonstrates

The 10¹³× gap in oxidation susceptibility is large enough that it cannot be closed by any conceivable improvement in formulation stabilizers, antioxidant additives, or packaging. Saturated-lipid alternatives such as the Arcturus LUNAR platform reduce but do not eliminate peroxidation because they retain some unsaturation. SHiP's architectural elimination of the substrate is, to our knowledge, the first proposed approach that closes the oxidation pathway entirely at the chemistry level rather than the engineering-control level.

The predicted shelf-life differential (median 58 months SHiP vs 0.8 months LNP at 25 °C) translates directly into the cold-chain economics described in the accompanying investor deck: a platform with 72× lower degradation rate at pharmacy temperature should be able to eliminate refrigerated transport for the subset of mRNA vaccines where this analysis applies.

4.2 What this analysis does NOT demonstrate

Several important limitations bound the strength of this conclusion:

  • These are first-order kinetic models, not molecular dynamics simulations. They do not capture late-stage autocatalysis in LNPs (which would make the LNP numbers worse, not better, so is conservative for our comparison). They do not capture cooperative aggregation in SHiP (which would make the SHiP numbers worse).

  • Potency is modeled as monotonic decay, not as a bimodal population where some particles are fully intact and others fully degraded. Real stability assays often show this bimodal behavior.

  • The model assumes the SHiP architecture works as designed. If the biomimetic silica shell cannot be deposited uniformly, or if the proton sponge fails to deliver mRNA to the cytosol, the predicted storage stability is academic. Those risks are addressed in the proposal's Phase 2 wet-lab plan, not here.

  • Rate constants for SHiP are derived from analogy, since no SHiP particle has been synthesized. They are calibrated against lyophilized mRNA precedents and biomimetic silica dissolution literature, but direct empirical rate constants require wet-lab work.

4.3 Experimental tests that would falsify this model

  • Accelerated stability study: synthesize pHK-mRNA polyplex, apply silica shell, lyophilize in trehalose, measure potency at 40 °C over 4 weeks. If potency drops >50% in 4 weeks, the model is wrong.
  • Isothermal DSC at 25 °C over 6 months to detect glass relaxation (molecular mobility) in the trehalose matrix. A Tg below expected indicates the model's moisture assumptions are off.
  • Peroxide value assay on SHiP vs LNP at 25 °C. SHiP should show no increase in peroxides over months; LNP should show measurable increase in weeks.

6. Next Steps

This report is Phase 1 of the SHiP computational validation plan. Phase 2 requires either HPC access for molecular dynamics simulations (pHK folding, mRNA-polymer electrostatic complexation) or a wet-lab partnership for empirical validation of the rate constants assumed here. Both options are described in the proposal's §9.

The most decisive next step is not more computation — it is synthesizing the simplest possible SHiP variant (pHK + mRNA, without silica shell) and measuring polyplex size by DLS. That single experiment, which a UBC mentor lab could execute in a week, would validate the most fundamental assumption of the platform and unlock seed-stage funding.
