The problem

mRNA vaccines live or die by the cold chain.

Current mRNA vaccines must be stored between −70 °C and −20 °C. The reason isn't engineering — it's chemistry. The ionizable lipids that make these vaccines work (SM-102, ALC-0315, DLin-MC3-DMA) all share a structural feature: unsaturated fatty tails with bis-allylic C–H bonds at roughly 75 kcal/mol. These bonds are what allow the lipid bubble to release its mRNA cargo inside cells. They are also what allow oxygen to silently tear the molecule apart on a pharmacy shelf.

Cooling slows oxidation. Antioxidants slow oxidation. Better packaging slows oxidation. Nothing stops it — because the substrate is built into the medicine. Every existing approach is engineering control over a chemistry that fundamentally wants to fail.

The insight

Don't slow the reaction.
Remove the substrate.

Every existing approach to mRNA vaccine stability tries to make oxidation happen more slowly. SHiP eliminates the substrate entirely. There is no oxidizable bond anywhere in the delivery vehicle — not in the polymer, not in the shell, not in the matrix. There is nothing for oxygen to attack, regardless of how much of it gets into the vial.

The architecture

Four layers, zero oxidizable bonds.

  1. 01
    pHK polymer core
    A saturated chain of poly(L-histidine-co-L-lysine). Lysine condenses mRNA via electrostatic collapse; histidine's imidazole (pKa ≈ 6.0) acts as a pH-activated switch that drives endosomal escape inside the cell.
  2. 02
    Biomimetic silica shell
    A ~5 nm glass coating that the polymer grows on its own surface — same biochemistry diatoms use. Silicon is already in its highest oxidation state. The shell is thermodynamically oxidation-terminal, then dissolves in the endosome to release cargo.
  3. 03
    Trehalose glass matrix
    The sugar tardigrades use to survive desiccation. At <1% residual moisture, forms a glassy solid (Tg > 70 °C) that immobilizes every particle and freezes molecular motion at room temperature.
  4. 04
    Mannose surface targeting
    PEG chains decorated with mannose. Recognized by CD206 on dendritic cells — antigen-presenting cells take SHiP up because they evolved to detect mannose-rich pathogens. The right cargo, to the right cell, by mimicry.
The science

For the formulation scientists in the room.

Each layer earns its place. Every bond was chosen against the same constraint: do its job without contributing a kinetically accessible C–H abstraction site. The depth is here for those who want it.

Why the bis-allylic bond is the architectural problem

Lipid peroxidation is a radical chain reaction that initiates at the weakest C–H bond in the molecule. In the unsaturated tails of SM-102, ALC-0315, and DLin-MC3-DMA, that's the bis-allylic position — a hydrogen on a carbon flanked by two C=C double bonds. Bond dissociation energy: ~75 kcal/mol. Once abstracted, the resulting carbon radical reacts with O₂ to form a peroxyl radical, which abstracts another bis-allylic hydrogen, and the chain propagates.

Saturated C–H bonds in aliphatic hydrocarbons sit at ~98 kcal/mol. That 23 kcal/mol gap corresponds to a rate ratio of order 10¹⁵ at ambient temperature. It's the same chemistry that makes flaxseed oil rancid in months and coconut oil shelf-stable for years. SHiP's saturated polymer, silica shell, PEG, mannose, and trehalose contain zero bis-allylic positions.

Why histidine specifically — the proton-sponge mechanism

Histidine's imidazole side chain has a pKa of approximately 6.0 — the lowest pKa of any standard amino acid. This is the single most important property in the entire SHiP design, because it sits in exactly the right window: neutral at extracellular pH (7.4), beginning to protonate in the early endosome (pH 6.0–6.5), fully protonated in the late endosome (pH 5.0).

When the polyplex is taken up by an APC, the V-ATPase pumps protons into the endosome. The histidine residues buffer these incoming protons; chloride ions flow in to balance charge; water follows by osmosis; the endosome swells and ruptures. Originally proposed by Behr (1995) for PEI; refined since for histidylated polylysine systems (Midoux & Monsigny, 1999).

Caveat: the proton sponge mechanism is contested in the literature. SHiP includes a fallback design with a GALA-style fusogenic peptide conjugate if proton-sponge alone proves insufficient.

The lysine dual role — silica nucleation and mRNA condensation

Lysine's ε-amine (pKa ≈ 10.5) is positively charged at physiological pH — which lets it grab and compact negatively charged mRNA via electrostatic collapse at N/P ratio ≈ 8.

The same primary amines also catalyze biomimetic silicification. Diatoms use this exact chemistry: silaffin peptides with surface amines deprotonate water at the Si surface and accelerate Si–O–Si condensation at neutral pH. When TMOS is added to the SHiP polyplex suspension, silica preferentially nucleates on the particle surface rather than in bulk solution. The particle grows its own armor.

The trehalose-glass / anhydrobiosis precedent

Tardigrades, brine shrimp, and resurrection plants survive near-complete desiccation by accumulating trehalose to roughly 20% of dry weight. The mechanism is twofold: water replacement at biomolecule surfaces (via hydrogen bonding through trehalose's eight hydroxyls), and vitrification — formation of an amorphous glass with Tg above 100 °C at low residual moisture.

Below Tg, molecular diffusion is arrested on pharmaceutical timescales. Particles can't aggregate, fuse, or react. Trehalose is non-reducing (won't Maillard with mRNA bases), GRAS, and already in approved biologics including Herceptin and Avastin. The novelty in SHiP isn't trehalose itself — it's combining trehalose vitrification with a non-oxidizable carrier so that the only remaining failure mode is bounded.

Key risks

Every risk we've identified, and what kills each one.

Naming risks well is a trust-builder. A proposal with no risks is a proposal that hasn't been thought through.

Proton sponge efficacy insufficient for cytosolic mRNA delivery
High
Fallback design incorporates a GALA-style or HA2-derived membrane-active peptide conjugate. Validated computationally in Phase 2 of the wet-lab plan.
Silica dissolution kinetics off-target in the endosome
Medium
Tune shell thickness in the 3–8 nm range; incorporate amine dopants to accelerate dissolution. Empirically characterizable at low cost.
Potency gap exceeds 10× vs current LNP
Medium
Pair platform with self-amplifying RNA payload to compensate. Cost-per-dose break-even at ~5× gap maintains commercial advantage.
Manufacturing reproducibility — silica shell uniformity batch-to-batch
Medium
Process development priority in Series A. Diatom-inspired biomimetic silicification has been demonstrated reproducibly in academic labs; industrial scale-up is the open question.
Amorphous silica immunogenicity
Low
Mesoporous silica nanoparticles are already in clinical trials. Precedent exists; characterization required but not novel.
Someone else publishes a similar approach first
Unknown
Phase 1 includes systematic literature review (2020–2026). Provisional patent filing scheduled before broader external outreach.

Kill criteria. If Phase 2 molecular dynamics shows that the proton sponge mechanism cannot rupture an endosomal vesicle even with the membrane-active peptide fallback, the project is terminated.
